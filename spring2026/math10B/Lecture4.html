Lecture 4: Swapping the Order of Integration and triple integrals

In multivariable calculus, the order of integration in an iterated integral can dictate whether a problem is solvable. Sometimes, a function lacks an elementary antiderivative with respect to one variable but becomes integrable if we switch the order of operations.

Example 1: Swapping Order with Linear Bounds

Express the following integral by reversing the order of integration: \[ \int_{0}^{8} \int_{y/2}^{4} xy \, dx \, dy \]

Step 1: Identify the original bounds of integration.

The region \( D \) is bounded by \( 0 \le y \le 8 \) and \( \frac{y}{2} \le x \le 4 \).

Visual Description: The domain is a triangle in the first quadrant of the \(xy\)-plane. It is bounded on the left by the line \( x = y/2 \) (or \( y = 2x \)), on the right by the vertical line \( x = 4 \), and at the bottom by the \(x\)-axis where \( y = 0 \). The triangle has vertices at \( (0,0) \), \( (4,0) \), and \( (4,8) \).

Step 2: Determine the new bounds for the \( dy \, dx \) order.

To integrate with respect to \( x \) on the outside, we find the total horizontal span of the region, which is \( 0 \le x \le 4 \). For a fixed value of \( x \), the vertical range for \( y \) starts at the \(x\)-axis (\( y = 0 \)) and goes up to the line \( y = 2x \).

Final Form: The integral with swapped order is: \[ \int_{0}^{4} \int_{0}^{2x} xy \, dy \, dx \]

Example 2: Evaluation with Trigonometric Functions

Evaluate the following integral by swapping the order: \[ \int_{0}^{1} \int_{y}^{1} \sin(x^2) \, dx \, dy \]

Remark: The function \( \sin(x^2) \) cannot be integrated with respect to \( x \) by elementary means. We must swap to integrate with respect to \( y \) first.

Step 1: Analyze the domain.

The original bounds are \( 0 \le y \le 1 \) and \( y \le x \le 1 \). This defines a triangle bounded by \( y = 0 \), \( x = 1 \), and \( y = x \).

Step 2: Change the order.

Reversing the order gives us the new bounds: \( 0 \le x \le 1 \) and \( 0 \le y \le x \).

Step 3: Evaluate.

\[ \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{x} \sin(x^2) \, dy \, dx = \int_{0}^{1} [y \sin(x^2)]_{y=0}^{y=x} \, dx \] \[ = \int_{0}^{1} x \sin(x^2) \, dx \]

Using \( u \)-substitution where \( u = x^2 \) and \( du = 2x \, dx \):

\[ \frac{1}{2} \int_{0}^{1} \sin(u) \, du = \frac{1}{2} [-\cos(u)]_{0}^{1} = \frac{1}{2}(1 - \cos(1)) \]
Example 2: Integrating \( e^{x^3} \) Over a Region

Evaluate the integral of \( e^{x^3} \) over the region \( D \) bounded by \( y = 0 \), \( x = 1 \), and \( y = x^2 \).

Remark: The function \( e^{x^3} \) cannot be integrated with respect to \( x \) by elementary means. We must choose the correct order to solve this.

Step 1: Describe the domain \( D \).

We can describe this region in two ways:

Option A: Treating \( x \) as independent

  • \( 0 \le x \le 1 \)
  • \( 0 \le y \le x^2 \)

Option B: Treating \( y \) as independent

  • \( 0 \le y \le 1 \)
  • \( \sqrt{y} \le x \le 1 \)
Visual Description: The region is the area "under" the parabola \( y = x^2 \) and "above" the \(x\)-axis, starting at the origin and ending at the vertical line \( x = 1 \).

Step 2: Evaluate using the solvable order.

If we use Option B (\( dx \, dy \)), we get \( \int_{0}^{1} \int_{\sqrt{y}}^{1} e^{x^3} \, dx \, dy \), which is not solvable because we cannot find \( \int e^{x^3} \, dx \). Using Option A (\( dy \, dx \)) instead:

\[ \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{x^2} e^{x^3} \, dy \, dx = \int_{0}^{1} [y e^{x^3}]_{y=0}^{y=x^2} \, dx \] \[ = \int_{0}^{1} x^2 e^{x^3} \, dx \]

Now we can use u-substitution: Let \( u = x^3 \), then \( du = 3x^2 \, dx \).

\[ = \left[ \frac{1}{3} e^{x^3} \right]_{x=0}^{x=1} = \frac{1}{3}e^1 - \frac{1}{3}e^0 = \frac{1}{3}(e - 1) \]

Triple Integrals

Definition: Triple Integrals

Triple integrals extend the concept of integration to three-dimensional space. They allow us to integrate a function \( f(x, y, z) \) over a solid region \( E \): \[ \iiint_{E} f(x, y, z) \, dV \]

Motivation and Visualization

While a single integral measures the area under a curve and a double integral measures the volume under a surface, a triple integral represents a hyper-volume. In practice, we use triple integrals to find properties of 3D solids such as mass or center of gravity. We focus on visualizing the domain of integration rather than the 4D function itself.

Concept: Fubini's Theorem for Triple Integrals

If the region \( R \) is a rectangular box \( [a,b] \times [c,d] \times [r,s] \), the triple integral can be evaluated as an iterated integral in any of the six possible orders (\( dx \, dy \, dz \), \( dz \, dy \, dx \), etc.).

Example 4: Triple Integral over a Rectangular Box

Evaluate \( \iiint_{R} xyz^2 \, dV \) over the region \( R = [0,1] \times [0,2] \times [0,3] \).

We choose the order \( dy \, dz \, dx \):

\[ \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} xyz^2 \, dy \, dz \, dx \]

Evaluation Steps:

  1. Integrate with respect to \( y \): \[ \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{3} \left[ \frac{1}{2}xy^2z^2 \right]_{y=0}^{y=2} \, dz \, dx = \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{3} 2xz^2 \, dz \, dx \]
  2. Integrate with respect to \( z \): \[ \int_{0}^{1} \left[ \frac{2}{3}xz^3 \right]_{z=0}^{z=3} \, dx = \int_{0}^{1} 18x \, dx \]
  3. Integrate with respect to \( x \): \[ [9x^2]_{x=0}^{x=1} = 9 \]
