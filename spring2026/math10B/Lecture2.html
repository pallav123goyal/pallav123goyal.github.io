Lecture 2: Iterated Integrals

Recall: Given a function \(f(x,y)\) and a region \(R\) in the \(xy\)-plane, the multiple integral \(\iint_{R}f(x,y) \, dA\) represents the volume enclosed below the graph of \(z=f(x,y)\) and above the \(xy\)-plane surrounded by the region \(R\).

Volume and Cross-Sections

Example 1: Geometric Approach

Compute \(\iint_{R}f(x,y) \, dA\) where \(f(x,y) = 1-x\) and \(R = [0,1] \times [0,1]\).

Visual Description: The region \(R\) is a square in the \(xy\)-plane with vertices at (0,0), (0,1), (1,0), and (1,1). In 3D space, the function \(z = 1-x\) represents a plane that intersects the \(z\)-axis at 1 and the \(x\)-axis at 1, forming a triangular prism over the square region \(R\).

We can think of this volume as half of a unit cube. Thus, the volume is:

\[ \text{Volume} = \frac{1}{2} \]

Alternatively, using the area of the triangular base multiplied by the length in the \(y\)-direction:

\[ \text{Volume} = \left( \frac{1}{2} \times 1 \right) \times 1 = \frac{1}{2} \]
Cavalieri's Principle: If we know the area of the cross-sections of a solid in a specific direction, we can integrate that area function to compute the total volume.
Example 2: Integration by Cross-Section

Suppose the area of a cross-section at any given \(x\) is \(A(x) = x^2 + 2\) for \(-1 \le x \le 4\). Compute the volume.

\[ \text{Volume} = \int_{-1}^{4} (x^2 + 2) \, dx \] \[ = \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} + 2x \right]_{-1}^{4} \] \[ = \left( \frac{64}{3} + 8 \right) - \left( \frac{-1}{3} - 2 \right) = \frac{95}{3} \]

Iterated Integrals over Rectangular Regions

To compute a double integral \(\iint_{R}f(x,y) \, dA\), we find the area of a cross-section at a given \(x\) by integrating with respect to \(y\), then integrate that result with respect to \(x\).

Example 3: Setting up the Iterated Integral

Compute the volume for \(f(x,y) = 1-x\) over \(R = [0,1] \times [0,1]\).

For a fixed \(x\), the area of the cross-section is \(\int_{0}^{1} (1-x) \, dy\). The total volume is:

\[ \text{Volume} = \int_{0}^{1} \left( \int_{0}^{1} (1-x) \, dy \right) \, dx \] \[ = \int_{0}^{1} [y - xy]_{0}^{1} \, dx \] \[ = \int_{0}^{1} (1-x) \, dx = \left[ x - \frac{x^2}{2} \right]_{0}^{1} = \frac{1}{2} \]
Example 4: Changing the Order of Integration

Compute \(\iint_{R} (x^2 + y^2 + 1) \, dA\) over \(R = [0,1] \times [0,2]\).

Step 1: Find the area of cross-section for a given \(y\):

\[ A(y) = \int_{0}^{1} (x^2 + y^2 + 1) \, dx = \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} + y^2x + x \right]_{0}^{1} = y^2 + \frac{4}{3} \]

Step 2: Integrate the cross-sectional area over the range of \(y\):

\[ \text{Volume} = \int_{0}^{2} \left( y^2 + \frac{4}{3} \right) \, dy = \left[ \frac{y^3}{3} + \frac{4y}{3} \right]_{0}^{2} = \frac{8}{3} + \frac{8}{3} = \frac{16}{3} \]
Fubini's Theorem: If \(f(x,y)\) is a continuous function on a rectangular region \(R\), then the order of integration does not matter: \[ \iint_{R} f(x,y) \, dA = \int_{a}^{b} \int_{c}^{d} f(x,y) \, dy \, dx = \int_{c}^{d} \int_{a}^{b} f(x,y) \, dx \, dy \]
Important Remark: Depending on the function \(f(x,y)\) and the region \(R\), it might be significantly easier to integrate in one order than the other.

Integration over General Regions

Example 5: Region between lines

Integrate \(f(x,y) = xy\) over the region enclosed by \(y = 2x+3\), \(y = x-5\), \(x = 0\), and \(x = 2\).

Visual Description: The region is bounded vertically by two linear functions: an upper line \(y = 2x+3\) and a lower line \(y = x-5\), between the vertical boundaries \(x = 0\) and \(x = 2\).
\[ \int_{0}^{2} \int_{x-5}^{2x+3} xy \, dy \, dx = \int_{0}^{2} \left[ \frac{xy^2}{2} \right]_{x-5}^{2x+3} \, dx \] \[ = \int_{0}^{2} \frac{x}{2} \left( (2x+3)^2 - (x-5)^2 \right) \, dx \] \[ = \int_{0}^{2} \frac{x}{2} (3x^2 + 22x - 16) \, dx = \int_{0}^{2} \left( \frac{3x^3}{2} + 11x^2 - 8x \right) \, dx \] \[ = \left[ \frac{3x^4}{8} + \frac{11x^3}{3} - 4x^2 \right]_{0}^{2} = 6 + \frac{88}{3} - 16 = \frac{58}{3} \]
