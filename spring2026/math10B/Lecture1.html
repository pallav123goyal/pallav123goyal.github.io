Lecture 1: Motivation and Introduction to Multiple Integrals

1. Motivation: Calculating Volume

To understand the need for multiple integrals, we start by looking at how we compute the volume of known shapes, such as a sphere of radius \(r\).

Visual Description: A coordinate system showing a circle centered at the origin with the equation \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\). The upper half of the circle is highlighted in red, representing the function \(y = \sqrt{r^2 - x^2}\). The points \((-r, 0)\) and \((r, 0)\) are marked on the x-axis.
Example: Volume of a Sphere via Disk Method

Consider a circle of radius \(r\) centered at the origin:

\[x^2 + y^2 = r^2\]

By solving for \(y\), the equation for the upper semi-circle (the red part) is:

\[y = \sqrt{r^2 - x^2} = f(x)\]

We can rotate this curve around the x-axis and use the disk method to find the volume.

Solution:

1. Set up the integral for the disk method:

\[\text{Volume} = \int_{-r}^{r} \pi [f(x)]^2 dx\]

2. Substitute \(f(x)\):

\[= \pi \int_{-r}^{r} (r^2 - x^2) dx\]

3. Evaluate the integral:

\[= \pi \left[ r^2x - \frac{x^3}{3} \right]_{-r}^{r}\]

4. Result:

\[= \frac{4\pi r^3}{3}\]

2. Arbitrary Shapes and Physics Applications

What if we need to compute the volume of more arbitrary or irregular shapes, like the radius of the Earth? This leads us to consider averages. We might guess that we need to compute the average distance of all points on the surface from a fixed point.

In physics, we see similar complexities. For two point particles at a distance \(s\), the gravitational force is given by:

\[F = \frac{k}{s^2}\]

where \(k\) is a constant. However, if we have non-point objects, calculating the force between them requires integrating over their entire mass distributions.

Introduction to Multiple Integrals

Generalizing the Single Integral

In single-variable calculus, given a function \(y = f(x)\), the integral \(\int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx\) is defined as the area under the curve between \(x = a\) and \(x = b\).

Visual Description: A standard 2D graph with a curve \(y = f(x)\). The area between the curve and the x-axis from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) is shaded, representing the integral.

We wish to generalize this concept to multivariable functions, such as \(f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 + 1\). In this case, the function represents a surface above the \(XY\)-plane.

Visual Description: A 3D coordinate system showing a surface (the graph of \(z = f(x, y)\)) suspended over a region \(R\) in the \(XY\)-plane. A vertical line connects a point in the region \(R\) to the surface.

Defining the Double Integral

Our goal is to define the double integral \(\iint_R f(x, y) dA\), where \(R\) is a region in the \(XY\)-plane.

Definition: Given a function \(f(x, y)\) and a region \(R\), the double integral

\[\iint_R f(x, y) dA\]

is the volume enclosed between the surface \(z = f(x, y)\) and the \(XY\)-plane over the region \(R\).

  • \(R\) is the domain of integration.
  • \(f(x, y)\) represents the height of infinitesimal cuboids.
  • \(dA\) represents the area of the base of these cuboids.

Intuition and Riemann Sums

Just as we approximate the area under a curve using thin rectangular strips, we can approximate the volume under a surface using cuboids.

The volume of a single cuboid is given by:

\[\text{Volume} = l \times b \times h\]

By chopping the region \(R\) into many small rectangles with area \(dA\) and multiplying by the height \(f(x, y)\), we create a Riemann sum that approximates the total volume.

Example 1: Integral of a Constant Function

Let \(f(x, y) = k\) for some constant \(k\). Let the region \(R\) be a rectangle defined by \(R = [a, b] \times [c, d]\).

Visual Description: A 2D plot of the \(XY\)-plane showing a shaded rectangular region \(R\). The rectangle is bounded by vertical lines \(x = a\) and \(x = b\), and horizontal lines \(y = c\) and \(y = d\).

Solution:

The graph of \(f(x, y) = k\) is a plane parallel to the \(XY\)-plane at height \(k\).

The integral \(\iint_R f(x, y) dA\) is equal to the volume of a cuboid with height \(k\) and base area determined by the rectangle \(R\).

1. Area of the base \(R\) = \((b - a)(d - c)\).

2. Height = \(k\).

3. Thus, \(\iint_R k dA = k(b - a)(d - c)\).
