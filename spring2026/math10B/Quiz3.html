Problem 1: (4 points) Set up a triple integral to compute the volume of the region enclosed by \(x=0\), \(y=2\), \(x-y=0\), \(z=x^2+y^2\), and \(x+y-z+4=0\). (You don't have to evaluate it.)

Solution: To find the volume, we identify the bounds for each variable. The region in the \(xy\)-plane is bounded by \(x=0\), \(y=2\), and the line \(y=x\). For the vertical bounds, we rewrite the plane equation as \(z = x+y+4\). Since the paraboloid \(z = x^2+y^2\) is the lower surface in this specific region, the setup is:

\[ V = \int_{0}^{2} \int_{0}^{y} \int_{x^2+y^2}^{x+y+4} dz \, dx \, dy \]

Problem 2: (2 points) Describe the region enclosed by the sphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=1\) in the first octant using spherical coordinates. That is, indicate the ranges for \(\rho\), \(\theta\), and \(\phi\).

Solution: In spherical coordinates, the unit sphere is defined by \(\rho = 1\). The "first octant" restriction limits our angles to the positive axes.

  • Radius: \(0 \le \rho \le 1\)
  • Azimuthal angle (in \(xy\)-plane): \(0 \le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}\)
  • Polar angle (from positive \(z\)-axis): \(0 \le \phi \le \frac{\pi}{2}\)

Problem 3: (4 points) Consider the region \(R\) enclosed between the circles \(x^2+y^2=4\) and \(x^2+y^2=9\) in the first quadrant. Set up an integral to compute \(\iint_{R} x \, dA\) in polar coordinates and evaluate it.

Warning: Don't forget the correction factor \(r\) that you need to multiply by when integrating with polar coordinates (\(dA = r \, dr \, d\theta\)).

Solution: In polar coordinates, the region \(R\) is bounded by \(r=2\) and \(r=3\), with \(\theta\) ranging from \(0\) to \(\frac{\pi}{2}\). Substituting \(x = r\cos\theta\):

\[ \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \int_{2}^{3} (r\cos\theta) r \, dr \, d\theta \]

Evaluating the radial part first:

\[ \int_{2}^{3} r^2 \, dr = \left[ \frac{1}{3}r^3 \right]_{2}^{3} = \frac{27}{3} - \frac{8}{3} = \frac{19}{3} \]

Then the angular part:

\[ \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \cos\theta \, d\theta = [\sin\theta]_{0}^{\pi/2} = 1 \]

The final value is \(\frac{19}{3} \times 1 = \frac{19}{3}\).
