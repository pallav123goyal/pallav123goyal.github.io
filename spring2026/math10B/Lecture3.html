Lecture 3: Integrating over Non-Rectangular Regions

Example 1: Integration over a Rectangular Region

Integrate the function \(f(x,y) = x^2 + y\) on the rectangular region with vertices \((0,0)\), \((3,0)\), \((0,2)\), and \((3,2)\).

Visual Description: A graph showing a shaded rectangular region in the first quadrant of the xy-plane. The rectangle is bounded by the x-axis from \(x=0\) to \(x=3\) and the y-axis from \(y=0\) to \(y=2\). The four corners are explicitly labeled at their respective coordinates.

In a rectangular region, the bounds for \(x\) and \(y\) are independent of each other. We can define the limits as:

\[0 \le x \le 3\]

\[0 \le y \le 2\]

The double integral is set up as follows:

\[\int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} (x^2 + y) \, dy \, dx\]

First, we integrate with respect to \(y\):

\[= \int_{0}^{3} \left[ x^2y + \frac{y^2}{2} \right]_{y=0}^{y=2} \, dx\]

\[= \int_{0}^{3} (2x^2 + 2) \, dx\]

Finally, we integrate with respect to \(x\):

\[= \left[ \frac{2x^3}{3} + 2x \right]_{x=0}^{x=3} = 18 + 6 = 24\]

Example 2: Integration over a Triangular Region

Integrate \(f(x,y) = x^2 + y\) over the triangular region with vertices \((0,0)\), \((3,0)\), and \((0,2)\).

Visual Description: A graph showing a triangular region bounded by the y-axis, the x-axis, and a downward-sloping line connecting the points \((0,2)\) and \((3,0)\). A vertical strip is shown within the triangle to illustrate that for a fixed value of \(x\), the range of \(y\) values starts at \(y=0\) and ends at the boundary line.

Important: Unlike rectangular regions, the bounds for \(y\) now depend upon the value of \(x\).

Let \(L\) be the line joining \((0,2)\) and \((3,0)\). We find the equation of line \(L\):

\[\frac{y - 2}{x - 0} = -\frac{2}{3} \implies y = -\frac{2}{3}x + 2\]

For any \(x\) such that \(0 \le x \le 3\), the bounds for \(y\) are:

\[0 \le y \le -\frac{2}{3}x + 2\]

We set up the integral:

\[\int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{-\frac{2}{3}x+2} (x^2 + y) \, dy \, dx\]

\[= \int_{0}^{3} \left[ x^2y + \frac{y^2}{2} \right]_{y=0}^{y=-\frac{2}{3}x+2} \, dx\]

Evaluating the inner limits results in the integrand:

\[x^2\left(-\frac{2}{3}x + 2\right) + \frac{1}{2}\left(-\frac{2}{3}x + 2\right)^2 = -\frac{2x^3}{3} + \frac{20x^2}{9} - \frac{4}{3}x + 2\]

Now, integrating with respect to \(x\):

\[\int_{0}^{3} \left( -\frac{2x^3}{3} + \frac{20x^2}{9} - \frac{4}{3}x + 2 \right) \, dx = \left[ -\frac{x^4}{6} + \frac{20x^3}{27} - \frac{2}{3}x^2 + 2x \right]_{0}^{3} = \frac{13}{2}\]

Alternative Approach: Changing the Order of Integration

We could also have solved this problem by letting \(y\) be the independent variable and having \(x\) depend on \(y\). Starting from the equation of the boundary line:

\[y = -\frac{2}{3}x + 2 \implies \frac{2}{3}x = 2 - y \implies x = -\frac{3}{2}(y - 2)\]

For any \(y\) such that \(0 \le y \le 2\), the bounds for \(x\) are \(0 \le x \le -\frac{3}{2}(y-2)\). The integral becomes:

\[\int_{0}^{2} \int_{0}^{-\frac{3}{2}(y-2)} (x^2 + y) \, dx \, dy\]

Example 3: Computing a Double Integral over a Triangular Region

Given \(f(x,y) = x + y\), compute the integral over the triangular region \(R\) bound by \((0,0)\), \((1,1)\), and \((2,0)\).

Visual Description: A triangle with a horizontal base on the x-axis from \(x=0\) to \(x=2\) and a peak at the point \((1,1)\). The left edge is the line \(y=x\) and the right edge is the line \(y=2-x\).

One approach is to split region \(R\) into two smaller triangles divided at \(x=1\). The boundaries are defined by line \(L_1\) (\(y=x\)) and \(L_2\) (\(y=2-x\)). The integral is the sum of two parts:

\[\int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{x} (x + y) \, dy \, dx + \int_{1}^{2} \int_{0}^{2-x} (x + y) \, dy \, dx\]

Another approach is to treat \(y\) as the independent variable. Here, \(0 \le y \le 1\). For a fixed \(y\), the bounds for \(x\) are found by solving the boundary equations for \(x\):

Left boundary: \(x = y\)

Right boundary: \(x = 2 - y\)

The integral becomes:

\[\int_{0}^{1} \int_{y}^{2-y} (x + y) \, dx \, dy\]

\[= \int_{0}^{1} \left[ \frac{x^2}{2} + xy \right]_{x=y}^{x=2-y} \, dy\]

\[= \int_{0}^{1} (2 - 2y^2) \, dy = \left[ 2y - \frac{2y^3}{3} \right]_{0}^{1} = \frac{4}{3}\]

Definition: Area via Double Integration

The area of a region \(R\) in the xy-plane is given by the double integral of the constant function 1 over that region:

\[\text{Area}(R) = \iint_{R} 1 \, dA\]
Example 4

Compute the area of the triangular region \(R\) from Example 3.

Using horizontal strips as before:

\[\text{Area}(R) = \int_{0}^{1} \int_{y}^{2-y} 1 \, dx \, dy\] \[= \int_{0}^{1} [x]_{y}^{2-y} \, dy = \int_{0}^{1} (2-y - y) \, dy\] \[= \int_{0}^{1} (2 - 2y) \, dy = [2y - y^2]_{0}^{1} = 2 - 1 = 1\]
