Lecture 7: Beyond Cartesian Coordinates

In Cartesian coordinates, we measure lengths relative to fixed \(x\) and \(y\)-axes. For example, a point might be labeled as \((10, 5)\) to denote its horizontal and vertical distance from the origin \((0,0)\).

Visual Description: A diagram showing the Cartesian coordinate system where a point is plotted at (10, 5). A horizontal line of length 10 and a vertical line of length 5 meet at the point, forming a rectangle with the axes.

Polar coordinates provide an alternative way to specify a point's location using a distance and an angle.

Definition: Polar Coordinates

A point in the plane is represented by the pair \((r, \theta)\) where:

  • \(r\): The distance of the point from the origin.
  • \(\theta\): The angle made by the point with the positive \(x\)-axis.

Converting Between Coordinate Systems

Given a point with Cartesian coordinates \((x, y)\), we can find its polar coordinates \((r, \theta)\) using the following relations derived from a right triangle:

\[r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\] \[\tan(\theta) = \frac{y}{x} \implies \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)\]

Conversely, if the polar coordinates \((r, \theta)\) are known, the Cartesian coordinates can be computed as:

\[x = r \cos(\theta)\] \[y = r \sin(\theta)\]
Visual Description: A right-angled triangle in the first quadrant. The hypotenuse is labeled \(r\), the horizontal leg is \(x\), and the vertical leg is \(y\). The angle between the \(x\)-axis and the hypotenuse is \(\theta\).

Integration in Polar Coordinates

Example 1: Circular Region

Integrate \(\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\) over the circular region \(x^2 + y^2 \le 4\).

Solution:

In Cartesian coordinates, the region is bounded by \(x = \pm 2\) and \(y = \pm \sqrt{4-x^2}\). The integral is:

\[\int_{-2}^{2} \int_{-\sqrt{4-x^2}}^{\sqrt{4-x^2}} \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \, dy \, dx\]

Using polar coordinates makes this much easier. The region \(x^2 + y^2 \le 4\) corresponds to a circle of radius 2:

  • \(0 \le r \le 2\)
  • \(0 \le \theta \le 2\pi\)
  • \(\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = r\)

The differential area element becomes \(dA = r \, d\theta \, dr\), where \(r\) is the correction factor. The integral becomes:

\[\int_{0}^{2} \int_{0}^{2\pi} r \cdot r \, d\theta \, dr = \int_{0}^{2} \int_{0}^{2\pi} r^2 \, d\theta \, dr\] \[= \int_{0}^{2} [r^2 \theta]_{\theta=0}^{\theta=2\pi} \, dr = \int_{0}^{2} 2\pi r^2 \, dr\] \[= \left[ \frac{2\pi r^3}{3} \right]_{r=0}^{r=2} = \frac{16\pi}{3} \text{}\]
Example 2: Annular Region

Set up an integral for a region \(S\) in the first quadrant bounded by \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\) and \(x^2 + y^2 = 9\).

Solution:

In polar coordinates, the bounds for this region are:

  • \(1 \le r \le 3\)
  • \(0 \le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}\)

The integral is set up as:

\[\iint_S f(r, \theta) \, r \, dr \, d\theta = \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \int_{1}^{3} f(r, \theta) \, r \, dr \, d\theta\]

In Cartesian coordinates, common way to handle this region is to calculate the integral over the larger region and subtract the smaller "hole." Since we are restricted to the first quadrant, we subtract the inner quarter-disk from the outer quarter-disk.

The setup in Cartesian coordinates is:

\[ \iint_S f(x,y) \, dA = \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{\sqrt{9-x^2}} f(x,y) \, dy \, dx - \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} f(x,y) \, dy \, dx \]
Visual Description: A graph of the first quadrant showing a "doughnut slice" (annulus). From x=0 to x=1, the vertical strips start at the inner circle curve and end at the outer circle. From x=1 to x=3, the vertical strips start at the x-axis and end at the outer circle.

There is one more way of setting this up in Cartesian coordinates: the region \(S\) may be split into two parts because the lower boundary changes at \(x=1\). The setup for the double integral is:

\[ \iint_S f(x,y) \, dA = \int_{0}^{1} \int_{\sqrt{1-x^2}}^{\sqrt{9-x^2}} f(x,y) \, dy \, dx + \int_{1}^{3} \int_{0}^{\sqrt{9-x^2}} f(x,y) \, dy \, dx \]

3D Coordinate Systems

1. Cylindrical Coordinates

Cylindrical coordinates \((r, \theta, z)\) extend polar coordinates into three dimensions by adding a standard \(z\)-axis.

Cylindrical to Cartesian: \[x = r \cos(\theta)\] \[y = r \sin(\theta)\] \[z = z\] Cartesian to Cylindrical: \[r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\] \[\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)\] \[z=z\]
Visual Description: A 3D coordinate system showing a point \((r, \theta, z)\). Its projection onto the \(xy\)-plane is the polar point \((r, \theta)\), and its height above the plane is \(z\).
Cylindrical Examples
  • Distance from Origin: The distance of a point \((r, \theta, z)\) from the origin is \(\sqrt{r^2 + z^2}\).
  • Surface \(r = 2\): This represents a cylinder of radius 2 centered around the \(z\)-axis.
  • Surface \(\theta = \pi/3\): This represents a half-plane starting from the \(z\)-axis.

2. Spherical Coordinates

Spherical coordinates \((r, \theta, \phi)\) describe a point based on its distance from the origin and two angles.

  • \(r\) (or \(\rho\)): Distance of the point from the origin.
  • \(\theta\): Angle made with the positive \(x\)-axis by the projection onto the \(xy\)-plane (same as polar/cylindrical).
  • \(\phi\): Angle made with the positive \(z\)-axis.
Spherical to Cartesian: \[x = r \sin(\phi) \cos(\theta)\] \[y = r \sin(\phi) \sin(\theta)\] \[z = r \cos(\phi)\] Cartesian to Spherical: \[r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}\] \[\theta = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)\] \[\phi = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{z}{r}\right)=\cos^{-1}\left(\frac{z}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}}\right)\]
Visual Description: A 3D coordinate system showing a point \((r, \theta, \phi)\). Its projection onto the \(xy\)-plane makes angle \(\theta\) with the positive \(x\)-axis, and it makes angle \(\phi\) with the positive \(z\)-axis.
Spherical Examples
  • Surface \(r = 2\): Represents a sphere of radius 2 centered at the origin.
  • Surface \(\theta = \pi/3\): This represents a half-plane starting from the \(z\)-axis.
  • Surface \(\phi = \pi/4\): Represents a cone opening upward from the origin.
