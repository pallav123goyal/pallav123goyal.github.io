Lecture 2: Iterated Integrals

Recall: Given a function \(f(x,y)\) and a region \(R\) in the \(xy\)-plane, the multiple integral \(\iint_{R} f(x,y) \, dA\) is the volume enclosed below the graph of \(z = f(x,y)\) and above the \(xy\)-plane surrounded by the region \(R\).

Volume Examples over Rectangular Regions

Example 1

Compute \(\iint_{R} f(x,y) \, dA\) where \(f(x,y) = 1 - x\) and \(R = [0,1] \times [0,1]\).

Note that the region \(R\) is a square with vertices \((0,0)\), \((0,1)\), \((1,0)\), and \((1,1)\).

Visual Description: In 3D space, the function \(f(x,y) = 1 - x\) represents the plane \(z + x = 1\). Over the unit square in the \(xy\)-plane, this forms a triangular prism. The surface starts at height \(z=1\) along the \(y\)-axis and slopes down to \(z=0\) at \(x=1\).
Solution

The integral represents the volume of a triangular prism, which is half of a unit cube.

\[ \text{Volume} = \frac{1}{2} \]

Iterated Integrals and Fubini's Theorem

To compute these integrals without relying on geometry, we use iterated integrals. For a rectangular region \(R = [a,b] \times [c,d]\), we can integrate with respect to one variable at a time.

Example 2: Iterated Computation

Compute \(\int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{1} (1-x) \, dy \, dx\).

Step-by-Step Solution

First, we evaluate the inner integral with respect to \(y\), treating \(x\) as a constant:

\[ \int_{0}^{1} (1-x) \, dy = [y - xy]_{0}^{1} = (1 - x) \]

Next, we integrate the result with respect to \(x\):

\[ \int_{0}^{1} (1-x) \, dx = \left[ x - \frac{x^2}{2} \right]_{0}^{1} = 1 - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2} \]

Calculating Volume via Cross-Sections

Example 3

Compute the volume under \(f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2 + 1\) over the rectangular region \(R = [0,1] \times [0,2]\).

Step 1: Find the Area of Cross-Section

For a given \(y\), the area of the cross-section in the \(x\)-direction is:

\[ A(y) = \int_{0}^{1} (x^2 + y^2 + 1) \, dx \] \[ A(y) = \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} + y^2x + x \right]_{0}^{1} = \frac{1}{3} + y^2 + 1 = y^2 + \frac{4}{3} \]
Step 2: Integrate Cross-Sections for Volume
\[ \text{Volume} = \int_{0}^{2} \left( y^2 + \frac{4}{3} \right) \, dy \] \[ \text{Volume} = \left[ \frac{y^3}{3} + \frac{4y}{3} \right]_{0}^{2} = \frac{8}{3} + \frac{8}{3} = \frac{16}{3} \]
Key Takeaway: If we had worked with cross-sections in the \(x\)-direction (integrating \(y\) first), we would get the same volume! \[ \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{2} (x^2 + y^2 + 1) \, dy \, dx = \frac{16}{3} \]
