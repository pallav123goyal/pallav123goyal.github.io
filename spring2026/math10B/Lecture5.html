Lecture 5: Triple Integrals

Example 1

Compute the triple integral \(\iiint_{D} (x+y) \, dV\) over the region \(D\) enclosed by the planes \(x=0\), \(y=0\), \(z=0\), and \(x+y+z=1\).

Visual Description: A tetrahedron in the first octant. The base is a triangle in the xy-plane bounded by the x-axis, y-axis, and the line \(y = 1-x\). The top surface is the plane \(z = 1-x-y\).

Step 1: Set up the bounds.
Substituting \(z=0\) into the plane equation \(x+y+z=1\) gives the boundary line \(x+y=1\) in the xy-plane. \[0 \le x \le 1\] \[0 \le y \le 1-x\] For a fixed point \((x,y)\) in the base, \(z\) varies from the xy-plane (\(z=0\)) to the surface: \[0 \le z \le 1-x-y\]

Step 2: Evaluate the integral.
\[ \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{1-x} \int_{0}^{1-x-y} (x+y) \, dz \, dy \, dx \] Evaluate with respect to \(z\): \[ \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{1-x} [z(x+y)]_{0}^{1-x-y} \, dy \, dx = \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{1-x} (1-x-y)(x+y) \, dy \, dx \] Let \(u = x+y\), then \(du = dy\): \[ \int_{0}^{1} \int_{x}^{1} (1-u)u \, du \, dx = \int_{0}^{1} [\frac{u^2}{2} - \frac{u^3}{3}]_{x}^{1} \, dx \] \[ = \int_{0}^{1} (\frac{1}{6} - \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{x^3}{3}) \, dx = [\frac{x}{6} - \frac{x^3}{6} + \frac{x^4}{12}]_{0}^{1} = \frac{1}{6} - \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{12} = \frac{1}{12} \]

Example 2

Compute the volume of the region enclosed between the coordinate planes and the planes \(y=x\), \(z=x+y\), and \(x=1\).

Visual Description: In the xy-plane, the region is a triangle bounded by the x-axis (\(y=0\)), the line \(y=x\), and the vertical line \(x=1\). The 3D volume sits above this triangle, bounded below by \(z=0\) and above by the plane \(z=x+y\).

Step 1: Determine the integration limits.
From the xy-plane projection: \[0 \le x \le 1\] \[0 \le y \le x\] The height \(z\) is bounded by: \[0 \le z \le x+y\]

Step 2: Set up the volume integral.
\[ V = \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{x} \int_{0}^{x+y} 1 \, dz \, dy \, dx \]

Step 3: Solve the inner integral (with respect to z).
\[ \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{x} [z]_{0}^{x+y} \, dy \, dx = \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{x} (x+y) \, dy \, dx \]

Step 4: Solve the middle integral (with respect to y).
\[ \int_{0}^{1} [xy + \frac{y^2}{2}]_{0}^{x} \, dx = \int_{0}^{1} (x(x) + \frac{x^2}{2}) \, dx \] \[ = \int_{0}^{1} (x^2 + \frac{1}{2}x^2) \, dx = \int_{0}^{1} \frac{3}{2}x^2 \, dx \]

Step 5: Solve the outer integral (with respect to x).
\[ [\frac{3}{2} \cdot \frac{x^3}{3}]_{0}^{1} = [\frac{x^3}{2}]_{0}^{1} = \frac{1}{2} \]

Example 3

Set up the triple integral for a region \(D\) that is a cylinder with radius 3 and height 4, centered around the z-axis.

Visual Description: A cylinder centered on the z-axis. The cross-section in the xy-plane is a circle of radius 3 (\(x^2 + y^2 = 9\)). The cylinder starts at the xy-plane (\(z=0\)) and extends to \(z=4\).

Step 1: Identify z-bounds.
The cylinder has a height of 4 and starts at the base \(z=0\): \[0 \le z \le 4\]

Step 2: Identify xy-bounds.
The circular base is \(x^2 + y^2 = 9\). Choosing \(x\) as the independent variable: \[-3 \le x \le 3\] Solving for \(y\): \[y^2 = 9 - x^2 \implies y = \pm\sqrt{9-x^2}\] \[-\sqrt{9-x^2} \le y \le \sqrt{9-x^2}\]

Step 3: Final Setup.
\[ \int_{0}^{4} \int_{3}^{3} \int_{-\sqrt{9-x^2}}^{\sqrt{9-x^2}} f(x,y,z) \, dy \, dx \, dz \]

Example 4

Identify and describe the region represented by the integral: \(\int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{x} \int_{x^2+y^2}^{2} xy \, dz \, dy \, dx\).

Step 1: Analyze the outer limits (xy-plane).
The limits \(0 \le x \le 1\) and \(0 \le y \le x\) define a triangular region in the xy-plane. The vertices of this triangle are \((0,0)\), \((1,0)\), and \((1,1)\).

Step 2: Analyze the inner limits (z-direction).
The surface \(z\) is bounded below by \(z = x^2 + y^2\), which is a circular paraboloid opening upwards. The surface \(z\) is bounded above by the horizontal plane \(z = 2\).

Step 3: Describe the solid.
The region is a vertical solid with a triangular base. The "floor" of the solid is a portion of a paraboloid, and the "ceiling" is a flat plane at height \(z=2\).

Example 5

Identify the region defined by the integral: \(\int_{0}^{1/2}\int_{0}^{3} \int_{-\sqrt{1-x^2}}^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} f(x,y,z)\, dy \, dz \, dx\).

The bounds suggest that z is an independent variable, and so, the above integral can be rewritten as \(\int_{0}^{1/2} \int_{-\sqrt{1-x^2}}^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \int_{0}^{3} f(x,y,z) \, dx \, dy \, dz\).

Step 1: Analyze the z-limits.
Since the limits for \(z\) are constant (\(0 \le z \le 3\)), the region is a vertical prism with a height of 3 units.

Step 2: Analyze the xy-region (the base).
The base is defined by: \[0 \le x \le 1/2\] \[-\sqrt{1-x^2} \le y \le \sqrt{1-x^2}\]

Step 3: Define the boundary curves.
In the xy-plane, the region is bounded by:

  • The y-axis (\(x=0\)) and the vertical line \(x=1/2\).
  • The bounds for y are arcs of the unit circle \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\) (rearranged from \(y = \pm\sqrt{1-x^2}\)).

Hence, the region is a prism whose base is the portion of the unit circle enclosed between the lines \(x=0\) and \(x=2\).
