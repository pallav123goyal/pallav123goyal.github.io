Problem 1 (4 points)

Compute the following integral: \[ \int_{0}^{1}\int_{\sqrt{y}}^{1} \cos(x^{3}) \, dx \, dy \]

Warning: The function \( \cos(x^{3}) \) is hard to integrate with respect to \( x \). Consider changing the order of integration.

Solution to Problem 1
Description of the region of integration: The original bounds are \( \sqrt{y} \le x \le 1 \) and \( 0 \le y \le 1 \). This describes a region in the first quadrant of the xy-plane bounded by the parabola \( y = x^2 \), the vertical line \( x = 1 \), and the horizontal line \( y = 0 \).

Step 1: Change the order of integration. By observing the region, we can see that if we integrate with respect to \( y \) first, the bounds for \( y \) go from 0 up to the curve \( y = x^2 \), and \( x \) ranges from 0 to 1.

The new integral is: \[ \int_{0}^{1}\int_{0}^{x^2} \cos(x^3) \, dy \, dx \]

Step 2: Evaluate the inner integral. Integrating with respect to \( y \): \[ \int_{0}^{x^2} \cos(x^3) \, dy = [y \cos(x^3)]_{0}^{x^2} = x^2 \cos(x^3) \]

Step 3: Evaluate the outer integral. Now we integrate with respect to \( x \) using substitution. Let \( u = x^3 \), then \( du = 3x^2 \, dx \), or \( \frac{1}{3}du = x^2 \, dx \). The bounds change from \( [0, 1] \) to \( [0^3, 1^3] = [0, 1] \).

\[ \frac{1}{3} \int_{0}^{1} \cos(u) \, du = \frac{1}{3} [\sin(u)]_{0}^{1} = \frac{1}{3} (\sin(1) - \sin(0)) \]

Final Answer: \( \frac{1}{3} \sin(1) \)

Problem 2 (6 points)

Find the volume of the region enclosed between the planes \( x=0 \), \( y=0 \), \( z=0 \), \( z=4 \), and \( x+y=6 \).

Hint: Draw a picture of the xy-plane to visualize the base of the solid.
Solution to Problem 2
Description of the region: The base of the solid in the xy-plane is a right triangle with vertices at (0,0), (6,0), and (0,6), bounded by the axes and the line \( y = 6 - x \). The height of the solid is constant at \( z = 4 \).

Step 1: Set up the double integral. The volume \( V \) of a solid with height \( z = f(x, y) \) over a region \( R \) is given by \( \iint_R f(x, y) \, dA \). Here, \( f(x, y) = 4 \).

Step 2: Determine integration bounds. Using the triangular base:

  • \( x \) ranges from 0 to 6.
  • For a fixed \( x \), \( y \) ranges from 0 to \( 6 - x \).

Step 3: Calculate the volume. \[ V = \int_{0}^{6} \int_{0}^{6-x} 4 \, dy \, dx \] \[ V = \int_{0}^{6} [4y]_{0}^{6-x} \, dx = \int_{0}^{6} 4(6-x) \, dx \] \[ V = \int_{0}^{6} (24 - 4x) \, dx = [24x - 2x^2]_{0}^{6} \] \[ V = (24 \cdot 6 - 2 \cdot 6^2) - 0 = 144 - 72 = 72 \]

Alternative Method: Since the height is constant, the volume is simply the Area of the Base multiplied by the Height. Area = \( \frac{1}{2} \cdot 6 \cdot 6 = 18 \). Volume = \( 18 \cdot 4 = 72 \).

Final Answer: 72 cubic units
