Review: Spherical Coordinates

In the previous lecture, we introduced spherical coordinates \((r, \phi, \theta)\). The relationship between Cartesian and spherical coordinates is defined by the following conversion formulas:

  • Distance from origin: \(r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}\)
  • Azimuthal angle: \(\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)\)
  • Polar angle: \(\phi = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{z}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}}\right)\)
Visual Description: A 3D coordinate system illustrating a point \(P\) with spherical coordinates \((r, \phi, \theta)\). The radius vector \(r\) connects the origin to \(P\). The polar angle \(\phi\) is the angle between the positive \(z\)-axis and the vector \(r\). The azimuthal angle \(\theta\) is the angle in the \(xy\)-plane between the positive \(x\)-axis and the projection of the vector \(r\) onto the plane.

To convert from spherical back to Cartesian coordinates, we use:

  • \(x = r \sin\phi \cos\theta\)
  • \(y = r \sin\phi \sin\theta\)
  • \(z = r \cos\phi\)

The standard ranges for these coordinates are \(0 \le r < \infty\), \(0 \le \phi \le \pi\), and \(0 \le \theta < 2\pi\).

Example: Visualizing Spherical Equations

Interpret what the following equations represent geometrically:

  1. \(r = 2\): This represents a sphere with radius 2 centered at the origin.
  2. \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{3}\): This represents a half-plane starting from the \(z\)-axis and making an angle of \(\frac{\pi}{3}\) with the positive \(x\)-axis.
  3. \(\phi = \frac{\pi}{3}\): This represents a cone opening upward along the \(z\)-axis, where the angle between the axis and the side of the cone is \(\frac{\pi}{3}\).
  4. The \(xy\)-plane: In spherical coordinates, the \(xy\)-plane is defined by \(\phi = \frac{\pi}{2}\).
  5. The \(xz\)-plane: This plane is represented by \(\theta = 0\) (for \(x > 0\)) and \(\theta = \pi\) (for \(x < 0\)).
  6. The \(yz\)-plane: This plane is represented by \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{2}\) and \(\theta = \frac{3\pi}{2}\).
  7. The \(z\)-axis: The positive \(z\)-axis is \(\phi = 0\), and the negative \(z\)-axis is \(\phi = \pi\).

Change of Variables and the Jacobian

When changing variables in a double integral, such as moving from Cartesian to polar coordinates, the differential area element changes. In Cartesian coordinates, \(dA = dx \, dy\). In polar coordinates, however, we use \(dA = r \, dr \, d\theta\), where \(r\) acts as a "correction factor."

Definition: The Jacobian: For a transformation defined by \(x = f(u, v)\) and \(y = g(u, v)\), the Jacobian \(\frac{\partial(x, y)}{\partial(u, v)}\) is the determinant: \[\frac{\partial(x, y)}{\partial(u, v)} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{vmatrix} = \frac{\partial x}{\partial u}\frac{\partial y}{\partial v} - \frac{\partial x}{\partial v}\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}\]
Theorem: Change of Variables: \[\iint_{R} f(x, y) \, dx \, dy = \iint_{S} f(x(u, v), y(u, v)) \left| \frac{\partial(x, y)}{\partial(u, v)} \right| \, du \, dv\]
Example 1: Integral over a Parallelogram

Evaluate \(\iint_{R} xy \, dA\) where \(R\) is the parallelogram with vertices \((0,0), (1,1), (1,4), (2,5)\).

Step 1: Determine boundary lines. The parallelogram is bounded by: \[y - x = 0, \quad y - x = 3\] \[y - 4x = 0, \quad y - 4x = -3\]

Step 2: Substitute. Let \(u = y - x\) and \(v = y - 4x\). Thus, \(0 \le u \le 3\) and \(-3 \le v \le 0\).

Step 3: Solve for \(x, y\). \[x = \frac{u - v}{3}, \quad y = \frac{4u - v}{3}\]

Step 4: Compute the Jacobian. \[\frac{\partial(x, y)}{\partial(u, v)} = \begin{vmatrix} 1/3 & -1/3 \\ 4/3 & -1/3 \end{vmatrix} = -\frac{1}{9} + \frac{4}{9} = \frac{1}{3}\]

Step 5: Evaluate the integral. \[\int_{-3}^{0} \int_{0}^{3} \left(\frac{u - v}{3}\right) \left(\frac{4u - v}{3}\right) \left| \frac{1}{3} \right| \, du \, dv\]
