Constructibility of Regular Polygons

Last time, we constructed an equilateral triangle. Today, we expand on the concept of regular polygons.

Definition: A regular \(n\)-gon is a shape with \(n\) equal sides and equal angles.
Theorem (Gauss, 1801; Wentzel, 1837): A regular \(n\)-gon can be constructed if and only if all odd prime factors of \(n\) are of the form \(2^{2^k} + 1\) for some \(k \ge 0\) (known as Fermat primes).

Examples of Fermat primes include:

  • For \(k=0\): \(2^{2^0} + 1 = 3\)
  • For \(k=1\): \(2^{2^1} + 1 = 5\)
  • For \(k=2\): \(2^{2^2} + 1 = 17\)

Corollary: A regular 7-gon cannot be constructed because 7 is not a Fermat prime.

Question: Uniqueness of Equilateral Triangles

Pick a point \(E\) inside an equilateral triangle \(ABC\). Is \(\triangle AEB\) equilateral?

Visual Description: A large triangle \(ABC\) is shown. A point \(E\) is located in the interior. Dashed lines connect \(E\) to vertices \(A\) and \(B\). Visually, the segments \(AE\) and \(BE\) are shorter than the base \(AB\).

One could argue that since \(E\) is inside the triangle, \(|AE| < |AB|\) and \(|BE| < |AB|\). However, this requires assumptions about the uniqueness of circles. It is often easier to argue using angles.

Congruence of Triangles

Definition: Two triangles \(ABC\) and \(A'B'C'\) are said to be congruent if all corresponding sides and angles are equal:
  • \(|AB| = |A'B'|\), \(|BC| = |B'C'|\), and \(|CA| = |C'A'|\)
  • \(\angle A = \angle A'\), \(\angle B = \angle B'\), and \(\angle C = \angle C'\)
We denote this as \(\triangle ABC \cong \triangle A'B'C'\).
Visual Description: Two identical triangles \(ABC\) and \(A'B'C'\) are positioned side-by-side. Corresponding sides are marked with matching tick marks (single, double, and triple dashes). Corresponding angles are marked with matching arc symbols.
Theorem (SAS - Side Angle Side): Suppose \(ABC\) and \(PQR\) are two triangles such that:
  • \(|AB| = |PQ|\)
  • \(\angle B = \angle Q\)
  • \(|BC| = |QR|\)
Then \(\triangle ABC \cong \triangle PQR\).

Properties of Equilateral Triangles

Proposition: All angles of an equilateral triangle are equal.
Proof of Angle Equality
Visual Description: An equilateral triangle \(ABC\) with vertices labeled. The interior angles at \(A, B, \text{and } C\) are labeled \(\alpha, \beta, \text{and } \gamma\) respectively.

Consider \(\triangle ABC\) where \(|AC| = |BC|\). If we flip the triangle along the middle (bisecting \(\angle C\)), we map the triangle to its mirror image \(\triangle BAC\). Under this transformation:

  1. \(|AC| = |BC|\) (Given)
  2. \(|BC| = |AC|\) (Given)
  3. \(\angle C = \angle C\) (Common angle)

Hence, by the SAS Theorem, \(\triangle ABC \cong \triangle BAC\). It follows that \(\angle A = \angle B\), or \(\alpha = \beta\).

By repeating this process and swapping the roles of \(A\) and \(C\), we can show that \(\angle A = \angle C\). Thus, \(\alpha = \beta = \gamma\).

Constructing a Midpoint

Goal: Given a line segment \(AB\), construct its midpoint \(E\).

Visual Description: A construction showing two circles centered at \(A\) and \(B\), both with radius \(AB\). The circles intersect at points \(C\) (above the segment) and \(D\) (below the segment). A vertical line segment connects \(C\) and \(D\), crossing the original segment \(AB\) at point \(E\).
Proof of Construction

Step 1: Show that \(\triangle CAD \cong \triangle CBD\).

Because \(\triangle ABC\) and \(\triangle ABD\) are equilateral by construction, we have \(|AC| = |BC|\) and \(|AD| = |BD|\). By the previous proposition, the base angles are equal. Using the SAS theorem, we establish that \(\triangle CAD \cong \triangle CBD\), which implies \(\angle ACE = \angle BCE\).

Step 2: Show that \(\triangle CAE \cong \triangle CBE\).

Using SAS again: we have \(|AC| = |BC|\), \(\angle ACE = \angle BCE\), and side \(CE\) is common. Therefore, \(\triangle CAE \cong \triangle CBE\). From this congruence, it follows that \(|AE| = |BE|\), proving that \(E\) is the midpoint of \(AB\).
